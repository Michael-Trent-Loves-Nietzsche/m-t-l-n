Three Books About Practical Maths

How To Lie With Statistics

Explains all the ways used to cynically or incompetently use numbers and data to... well... lie. Thorough and exlpains things simply. Probably best paired with How to make the world add up so you don’t get too downhearted about things. Remember, Tim Harford’s adage is true: it’s easy to lie with statistics but even easier to lie without them.

How To Make The World Add Up

A good book for sure but one you can miss if you’re already familiar with Tim Harford’s work on Radio 4 and his podcasts. This explains how numbers can be used to make sense of current events and politics.

Fooled By Randomness

The third in the trio I’d recommend. This is more of a philosophical look but goes into the maths just enough for my liking. Does what it says on the tin and if like me you’re thinking “does this need to be this long to get the point across” the answer is maybe not but still enjoyable all the way though.

Present Shock (Douglas Rushkoff)

This is a book I would actually not recommend. I didn’t manage to finish it and I’m probably not going to. I do think there is a lot of value in the what Rushkoff says in interviews and on his podcast (Team Human) but his writing style didn’t give the same energy. Also, I think it’s worth pointing out that Rushkoff is a cultural critic and not a cut and dried historian or sociologist so I would say his ideas are at best ‘something to think about’ and should not be taken as facts.
